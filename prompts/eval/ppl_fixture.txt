The history of computing spans several centuries, beginning with early mechanical calculators and progressing through electromechanical systems to the electronic computers of the mid-twentieth century.
Charles Babbage designed the Analytical Engine in the 1830s, a proposed general-purpose mechanical computer that was never completed during his lifetime but anticipated many features of modern machines.
Ada Lovelace, working with Babbage, wrote what is often considered the first algorithm intended to be processed by a machine, earning her recognition as an early figure in the history of programming.
The invention of the transistor in 1947 at Bell Labs by John Bardeen, Walter Brattain, and William Shockley made it possible to build reliable electronic devices that were much smaller than vacuum-tube systems.
Integrated circuits, developed independently by Jack Kilby and Robert Noyce in the late 1950s, allowed many transistors to be placed on a single chip, and this density has continued to double roughly every two years, a trend summarized by Moore's Law.
The first programmable electronic general-purpose computer, ENIAC, was completed in 1945 at the University of Pennsylvania and was used primarily for calculating artillery firing tables for the United States Army.
Early programming was done by physically rewiring the machine, a labor-intensive process that motivated the development of stored-program architectures in which both instructions and data live in memory.
The stored-program concept is often associated with the EDVAC report of 1945, and its practical realization is sometimes credited to the Manchester Baby of 1948 and the EDSAC of 1949.
High-level programming languages began to appear in the 1950s, with FORTRAN introduced by IBM in 1957 for scientific computation and COBOL standardized shortly afterward for business data processing.
The Unix operating system was developed at Bell Labs beginning in 1969 and has influenced nearly every subsequent operating system through its design choices, its portability, and the C programming language that was created alongside it.
The personal computer revolution of the late 1970s and early 1980s brought computing into homes and small businesses, with machines such as the Apple II, the Commodore 64, and the IBM PC becoming cultural icons.
The graphical user interface, pioneered at Xerox PARC and popularized by the Apple Macintosh in 1984, replaced line-based terminals with windows, icons, and a mouse-driven cursor.
The World Wide Web, proposed by Tim Berners-Lee in 1989 and released to the public in 1991, transformed the internet from a network used mainly by researchers into a platform for global commerce and communication.
The rise of mobile computing accelerated after the introduction of the iPhone in 2007, placing a powerful networked computer in the pockets of hundreds of millions of people within a decade.
Cloud computing abstracted hardware ownership behind a metered service model, allowing small teams to run workloads that previously required a substantial capital investment in servers and cooling.
Deep learning, a branch of machine learning based on artificial neural networks with many layers, surged to prominence in the early 2010s after advances in hardware, datasets, and training techniques.
Graphics processing units, originally designed to accelerate video games, turned out to be well suited for the linear algebra operations at the heart of neural network training and inference.
Transformer models, introduced in a 2017 paper titled Attention Is All You Need, became the dominant architecture for language and multimodal tasks within a few years of their publication.
Large language models trained on broad web-scale data demonstrated strong zero-shot and few-shot performance, reshaping expectations about what a single model could do without task-specific fine-tuning.
The practice of quantization reduces the numeric precision of neural network weights and activations, trading a small amount of accuracy for significant memory and sometimes compute savings.
Weight-only quantization stores weights at low precision but performs the matrix multiplication in higher precision after dequantizing, a common approach on hardware that lacks native low-precision kernels.
The Apple M-series chips integrate CPU, GPU, and a unified memory pool on a single die, giving the graphics pipeline direct access to the same memory used by the operating system and applications.
On Apple silicon, the Metal performance shaders framework provides GPU-accelerated primitives that are broadly useful for machine learning workloads but do not currently include native int8 or int4 matrix multiplication.
The PyTorch framework introduced an MPS backend that executes tensor operations on the Apple GPU through Metal, providing a path for neural network training and inference on laptops without a discrete GPU.
Benchmarking an inference system honestly requires measuring both throughput and tail latency under realistic arrival patterns, because synthetic closed-loop runs can hide queueing problems that show up in production.
Continuous batching is a scheduling technique in which newly arriving requests can join an in-progress batch on a per-iteration basis, improving hardware utilization compared with static batching approaches.
Prefix caching retains the key-value tensors produced during prefill so that later requests sharing the same token prefix can skip redundant computation, a common optimization for chat workloads with long system prompts.
Prometheus is an open-source monitoring system that scrapes time-series metrics from services at regular intervals and stores them in a local database, with a flexible query language called PromQL for aggregation and alerting.
Grafana is a widely used dashboarding tool that visualizes metrics from sources such as Prometheus, with support for alerting, templating, and panel provisioning through files checked into version control.
The combination of Prometheus and Grafana has become a standard observability stack for many engineering teams, including those running small self-hosted services and those operating very large distributed systems.
Evaluating a language model on a held-out corpus by computing perplexity, the exponential of the average negative log-likelihood per token, gives a coarse but comparable measure of how well the model predicts text.
Perplexity is sensitive to tokenizer choice and corpus composition, so absolute numbers across papers are not always directly comparable, but relative changes between two versions of the same model are meaningful.
A completion quality task like HellaSwag measures common-sense reasoning by asking the model to choose the most likely continuation of a short narrative from four plausible options.
Smaller models often score near chance on HellaSwag, making it less useful as a sole indicator at the sub-billion-parameter scale, though it remains informative for relative comparisons between quantization levels of the same base model.
Open-source benchmarking frameworks such as lm-evaluation-harness bundle many standard tasks behind a common interface, which reduces the effort of running a fair comparison across models and checkpoints.
The maximum sequence length supported by a transformer is bounded by the positional encoding scheme and the memory available for the attention cache, both of which have been pushed substantially higher over the last few years.
Rotary position embeddings, commonly abbreviated RoPE, apply a rotation in the complex plane to query and key vectors based on their position, and they interact in useful ways with a technique known as position interpolation.
Grouped-query attention reduces the number of distinct key and value projection heads so that memory for the KV cache grows more slowly with sequence length, a choice adopted by several recent open-weight model families.
The TinyLlama project trained a small language model based on the Llama 2 architecture on a large quantity of natural text, producing checkpoints that are useful for experimentation on consumer hardware.
Experimenting on a laptop imposes constraints that encourage disciplined measurement: memory is tight, batch sizes are small, and the GPU has to be shared with the operating system's compositor.
Those constraints are not purely negative, because they force an implementer to understand where time is actually being spent rather than relying on the cushion of a cluster to mask inefficiencies.
